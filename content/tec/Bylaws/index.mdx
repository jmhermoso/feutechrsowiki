This section serves as the operational manual for the creative pipeline. It moves from high-level discovery to the granular details of technical handover. By following this architecture, the department maintains a standardized quality of output regardless of which officer is leading the project.

## 1.1 Pre-Pre: The Discovery Phase

The discovery phase acts as the filter for the entire department. It is designed to prevent resource mismanagement by ensuring a project is viable before any technical labor begins.

### Understanding the Request and Client Intake

The process is initiated via a formal request. To maintain clear communication, the production lead must identify a **Single Point of Contact (SPOC)**. This prevents "instruction creep" where multiple voices from a client organization provide conflicting directions.

| Requirement | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| **SPOC Identification** | Prevents conflicting revisions and communication bottlenecks. |
| **Core Objective** | Defines if the content is for archival, promotion, or live broadcast. |
| **Visual References** | 2-3 external links to align on the expected "look and feel." |

**🎯 Action Checklist:**
- [ ] Schedule intake meeting with client (within 24 hours of request)
- [ ] Identify and document SPOC name, email, phone
- [ ] Ask: "What happens if this project succeeds?"
- [ ] Collect 2-3 reference videos/images
- [ ] Document: Core objective, deliverable format, target audience

---

### Scope Definition and Limitations

With the objective clear, the lead must define the boundaries of the project. Establishing the "Creative Freedom" vs. "Client Mandates" at this stage prevents friction during the editing process.

**The Creative Freedom Matrix:**

| Zone | Who Decides | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Client Mandate** | Client only | Brand colors, messaging, legal compliance |
| **Collaborative** | Lead + Client | Shot composition, narrative structure, pacing |
| **Creative Freedom** | Department only | Technical crew, specific equipment, color grading approach |

> **Critical:** A critical component here is the **Scope Creep Policy**, which stipulates that any request changing the workload by more than 20% after initial approval must be processed as a separate project.

**💡 Real-World Example:**
- **Original Scope:** 3-minute promotional video (8 hrs labor estimated)
- **Mid-Production Add:** Client requests behind-the-scenes B-roll (+4 hrs) = 50% increase
- **Action:** Stop work. Process as Change Order or separate project.

---

### Research and Feasibility

The final step of the discovery phase is a cold assessment of the department's current capabilities. This involves two critical audits:

<Tabs items={['Hardware Audit', 'Location Audit']}>
  <Tab value="Hardware Audit">
    **Hardware Audit Checklist:**
    - [ ] High-speed cameras available for slow-motion?
    - [ ] Specific lenses (macro, ultra-wide, telephoto) in stock?
    - [ ] Audio gear meeting client's acoustic requirements?
    - [ ] Lighting kit sufficient for venue size and tone?
    - [ ] Backup power (batteries, generators) available?
    - [ ] Storage media (SSD, SD cards) sufficient for expected footage volume?
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Location Audit">
    **Location Audit Checklist:**
    - [ ] Power: How many amps available? Where are circuits?
    - [ ] Acoustics: Is the space naturally reverberant (cathedral) or deadened (warehouse)?
    - [ ] Access: Loading dock? Elevators? Stairwell dimensions for gear?
    - [ ] Environmental: Weather patterns? Noise permits? Parking?
    - [ ] Safety: Hazards (trip wires, heights, chemical exposure)?
    - [ ] Insurance: Are there location-specific liability requirements?
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### 🟢 Pre-Pre FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="How do we handle a client who refuses to provide a SPOC?">
    Inform the client that without a single point of contact, the production timeline will be extended to account for conflicting revisions, and the "Kill Switch" policy will be strictly enforced. Document this conversation.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if the request exceeds our current equipment's capabilities?">
    Flag this in the feasibility report. The project may proceed only if the client provides the budget for rentals or accepts a lower technical tier of delivery. Example: "We can deliver 4K at 60fps with rental gear (+$2,500), or 1080p 60fps with in-house gear."
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Who owns the feasibility decision?">
    The Production Lead makes the final call. If uncertain, escalate to the Department Head.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>

## 1.2 Pre-Production: The Blueprint

Pre-production is the phase where abstract ideas are converted into actionable technical data. Without a blueprint, on-site execution becomes reactive rather than proactive.

### Creative Assets and Storyboarding

Visualizing the project is the most effective way to save time during a shoot. The team begins with three sequential deliverables:

<Tabs items={['Logline', 'Moodboarding', 'Storyboarding']}>
  <Tab value="Logline">
    **The Logline (1 sentence)**
    
    A single sentence that keeps the narrative laser-focused.
    
    - ❌ Bad: "A video about our company culture"
    - ✅ Good: "A day-in-the-life following Sarah from HR as she onboards a new employee and discovers an unexpected mentorship"
    
    **Tips:**
    - Answer: "What is the core emotional journey?"
    - Use active verbs (following, discovering, building, revealing)
    - Keep it under 20 words
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Moodboarding">
    **Moodboarding (5-10 reference images)**
    
    Aligns aesthetic choices like color palettes, lighting style, and tone.
    
    - Collect images from Unsplash, Pinterest, or films
    - Organize by: "Dark & moody" vs. "Bright & energetic"
    - Label specific elements: "This lighting" or "This color grade"
    
    **Deliverable:**
    - Create a shared Pinterest board or Figma file
    - Include 8-10 images minimum
    - Add notes on each image explaining what resonates
    - Share with client for approval before proceeding
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Storyboarding">
    **Storyboarding (Shot-by-shot sequence)**
    
    Provides a sequential map of every planned shot. Use:
    - Rough sketches (stick figures OK!)
    - Numbered sequence (Shot 1, Shot 2, etc.)
    - Camera direction (Wide, Medium, Close-up)
    - Brief description of action
    
    **Format:**
    - Digital (Figma, Adobe XD) or physical (paper thumbnails)
    - One shot per panel
    - Include: Shot number, shot type, duration, brief description
    - Minimum 8 shots for any project
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

**📋 Pre-Production Deliverables Checklist:**
- [ ] Logline approved by client
- [ ] Moodboard shared and signed off
- [ ] Storyboard with shot descriptions (minimum 8 shots)
- [ ] All creative assets in shared folder
- [ ] Client feedback incorporated

---

### Logistics and Planning

Technical planning involves a methodical walkthrough of the physical space and coordination of all personnel.

**Venue Scout Walkthrough:**
During the scout, the lead walks through the location with a checklist and **takes photos/video at each camera position**. Mark:
- Primary camera position (wide establish shot)
- Secondary angles (medium, close-up, profile)
- Audio recording locations (where is ambient noise loudest/quietest?)
- Talent traffic flow (where will actors stand, walk, sit?)
- Power outlet locations and circuit capacity
- Cable run paths (avoid tripping hazards)

**Parallel: Talent Sourcing**
- Confirm on-camera talent availability 2 weeks prior
- Provide wardrobe brief: "Earth tones, layers, movement-friendly"
- Send call time and location (with parking details)
- Confirm contact phone numbers day-before

---

### Equipment Manifest

The final output of logistics is the **Equipment Manifest**, an itemized checklist of every component required for the shoot. Every item must be accounted for.

**Sample Equipment Manifest Format:**

| Category | Item | Qty | Spec | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Camera** | RED Komodo | 1 | 6K RAW | Primary A-camera |
| **Lens** | Canon EF 24-70mm | 2 | f/2.8 L | A + B cam |
| **Audio** | Rode Wireless Go II | 3 | 2.4 GHz | Talent + boom |
| **Power** | V-Mount Battery | 6 | 7200mAh | 8hr shoot = 4 needed |
| **Storage** | CFast Card | 8 | 128GB | 6K RAW eats ~50GB/hr |

✅ **Best Practice:** Create manifest 2 weeks before shoot. Order any rentals immediately. Check every item 48 hours prior (test batteries, verify lenses focus smoothly).

### 🟢 Pre-Production FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Why do we need a moodboard if the client already gave us a reference video?">
    A reference video shows what someone *else* did; a moodboard shows what *we* will do. It translates the client's vague "vibe" into specific, measurable targets our team can hit during the shoot (e.g., "Key light at 45°, warm color grade, shallow DOF").
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Is a venue scout necessary for a location we have used before?">
    **Yes.** Facilities change—power outlets may be broken, new furniture may obstruct previous angles, or the client may have modified the space. Always verify 24–48 hours prior.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if the client rejects the storyboard?">
    Document their feedback. Revise and resubmit. This is the cheapest time to make changes; changes on-set cost time and crew morale.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>

### Crew Management

Between technical phases, the lead must actively manage **Rest and Meal Breaks**. Production efficiency drops significantly after five hours of continuous labor; scheduling breaks is a technical necessity to prevent mistakes.

<Tabs items={['Welfare Standards', 'Break Schedule Example', 'Catering Guide']}>
  <Tab value="Welfare Standards">
    **Crew Welfare Standards:**
    - No more than 5 consecutive hours of work without a break
    - Provide snacks every 2 hours (water, fruit, granola bars)
    - Lunch break minimum 30–60 minutes (preferably staggered)
    - Shade/shelter during breaks
    - Clear expectations on wrap time (don't surprise crew with 2-hour overtimes)
    - Communicate schedule changes immediately
    - Never ask crew to work through a planned break without discussion
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Break Schedule Example">
    **Sample 10-Hour Shoot Schedule:**
    
    - **6:00 AM** Load-In (30 min)
    - **6:30 AM** Tech Setup
    - **9:00 AM** **First Break** (15 min snack)
    - **9:15 AM** Resume shooting
    - **12:00 PM** **Lunch Break** (60 min)
    - **1:00 PM** Resume shooting
    - **3:00 PM** **Afternoon Break** (15 min snack)
    - **3:15 PM** Resume shooting
    - **4:30 PM** Strike begins (crew gets paid rest)
    - **5:30 PM** Wrap
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Catering Guide">
    **Craft Services Recommendations:**
    
    **Morning (Arrival):**
    - Water stations (essential)
    - Coffee/tea
    - Light pastries or granola bars
    
    **Lunch (Staggered):**
    - Hot and cold options
    - Vegetarian choices
    - Hydration (water + electrolytes)
    - Budget: $15–25 per person
    
    **Afternoon Snack:**
    - Fresh fruit
    - Nuts or protein bars
    - Cold water is critical in heat
    
    **Budget Estimate:** $150–250 for crew of 8, full day
  </Tab>

### 🟢 On-Site FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="What is the priority if we are running behind schedule?">
    Refer to the "Priority" column in the Shotlist. Drop everything marked 🟢 Bonus, then 🟡 Nice to Have. Focus 100% on 🔴 Must Have shots. If you complete those, you've delivered.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Who is responsible for gear safety on-site?">
    Every crew member is responsible for their immediate station (don't leave cameras unattended). The Lead is responsible for the final "Litter Sweep" and inventory check during Strike phase.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can we keep shooting if the talent is exhausted?">
    No. Tired talent = sloppy performance. Take a break. A 15-minute rest often yields better takes than pushing through.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>

## 1.4 Post-Production and Publication

Post-production focuses on data integrity and the final departmental "handshake" that brings the content to the audience.

### File Organization and Ingest

The workflow begins with **Data Ingest**. Footage must be moved to a master drive with an immediate secondary backup.

<Tabs items={['Naming Convention', 'Folder Structure', 'Backup Protocol']}>
  <Tab value="Naming Convention">
    **Standard File Naming Convention:**
    ```
    YYYYMMDD_ProjectName_ClipDescription_V01
    Example: 20240315_iTamaraws_TalentWalkIn_V01.mov
    ```
    
    **Rules:**
    - **Date:** Always YYYYMMDD format (sortable)
    - **Project Name:** No spaces, use CamelCase or underscores
    - **Description:** Brief, descriptive (not "Footage_01" but "TalentWalkIn" or "ProductShot")
    - **Version:** V01, V02, etc. if there are re-exports
    - **File Type:** Match the actual format (.mov, .mp4, .wav, etc.)
    
    **Bad Examples:**
    - "footage.mov"
    - "take_5_final_FINAL_FINAL.mov"
    - "Video from March"
    
    **Good Examples:**
    - "20240315_iTamaraws_EstablishShot_V01.mov"
    - "20240315_iTamaraws_DialogueA_V02.mov"
    - "20240315_iTamaraws_BRoll_Exterior_V01.mov"
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Folder Structure">
    **Folder Structure (Sacred):**
    ```
    20240315_iTamaraws_Campaign/
    ├── 01_RAW/
    │   ├── Camera_A/
    │   ├── Camera_B/
    │   └── Audio_Stems/
    ├── 02_AUDIO/
    │   ├── Dialogue/
    │   ├── SFX/
    │   └── Music/
    ├── 03_ASSETS/
    │   ├── Graphics/
    │   ├── Logos/
    │   └── Stills/
    ├── 04_PROJECT_FILES/
    │   ├── DaVinci/
    │   └── Premiere/
    └── 05_EXPORTS/
        ├── Master_4K/
        ├── Web_1080p/
        └── Social_9_16/
    ```
    
    **Rule:** Any new editor must understand the project structure within 30 seconds of opening the folder.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Backup Protocol">
    **Critical: 3-2-1 Backup Rule**
    
    - **3** copies of data (original + 2 backups)
    - **2** different storage types (SSD + external HDD)
    - **1** offsite backup (cloud or secondary location)
    
    **Implementation:**
    1. Copy footage to Master Drive (USB 3.0 external SSD) — same day
    2. Immediately copy to Secondary Backup (another external HDD) — same day
    3. Upload critical assets to cloud (Google Drive, Dropbox) within 48 hours
    4. Verify all backups by spot-checking files — same week
    
    **Never:**
    - Keep raw footage on a single hard drive
    - Leave footage on camera SD cards after offloading
    - Assume a cloud backup replaces local redundancy
    - Store backups in the same location as the original
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### The Editing Workflow

Editing proceeds in two major stages, each with specific criteria for "lock."

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Stage 1: Assembly Cut (Story Focus)">
    - **Goal:** Establish narrative flow and pacing
    - **Deliverable:** 90% complete rough edit with all footage
    - **Client involvement:** Reviews and approves story direction
    - **Typical duration:** 3–5 business days
    - **Success metric:** "Does the story make sense?"
    - **Typical feedback:** Plot structure, pacing, shot selection
    - **Do not include yet:** Color grading, sound design, graphics
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stage 2: Fine Cut (Polish Focus)">
    - **Goal:** Color grading, sound design, graphics integration
    - **Deliverable:** Final, publication-ready master
    - **Client involvement:** Locked after client final approval
    - **Typical duration:** 5–10 business days
    - **Success metric:** "Is every frame broadcast-quality?"
    - **Typical feedback:** Color tone, music placement, graphic timing
    - **Include:** All color grades, sound mix, motion graphics, transitions
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Client Feedback Process">
    - **All feedback logged** in shared Google Doc
    - **Use timestamped comments** (e.g., "0:15 - Color is too blue, should be warmer")
    - **Editor responds** with solution or clarifying questions
    - **Limit revision rounds** to 2–3 per stage (more = scope creep)
    - **Track turnaround time:** Aim for 48-hour editor responses
    - **Freeze feature:** Once approved, no new feedback accepted
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>

### The Publication Handshake

Once the edit is finalized, Production facilitates handover to the Publications Department. This is not a hand-off—it's a **collaborative handshake**.

<Tabs items={['Handover Package', 'Technical Briefing', 'Caption Verification']}>
  <Tab value="Handover Package">
    **The Handover Package includes:**
    
    1. **High-Quality Master** (4K, ProRes, uncompressed audio)
       - Upload to shared drive
       - File size: typically 30–100 GB
       - Keep in secure, backed-up location
    
    2. **Social Cut-downs** (16:9 → 9:16 vertical for Instagram/TikTok)
       - Separate file optimized for mobile
       - Include safe zones (avoid overlays in edges)
       - Compressed for web (H.264, ~50 MB for 1 min video)
    
    3. **Technical Briefing Doc** 
       - See "Technical Briefing" tab
    
    4. **Caption/Subtitle File** (SRT format)
       - Machine-readable subtitle file
       - For accessibility and international reach
       - Include English + local language options
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Technical Briefing">
    **Technical Briefing Template:**
    ```
    PROJECT: iTamaraws Winter Campaign
    DELIVERY DATE: March 20, 2024
    EDITOR: [Name]

    TALENT NAMES (spellings to use):
    - Sarah Chen (not Sara, not Shaw)
    - Dr. Juan Dela Cruz (include title)

    LEGAL/TECHNICAL WARNINGS:
    - Contains rapid cuts (potential epilepsy trigger) - flag in caption
    - Music licensed until Dec 31, 2024 - refresh after that date
    - B-roll from Unsplash (free, no attribution required)

    COLOR SPACE: Rec. 709
    AUDIO: 5.1 Surround (also includes stereo mixdown)
    SUBTITLES: English + Filipino (both included in Master)

    NOTES:
    - Master file is 45 GB - allow 30 min for download
    - Social cut-downs are optimized for mobile (safe zone respected)
    - All graphics are motion graphics (no static text that might be unreadable on mobile)
    ```
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Caption Verification">
    **The Caption Verification Process:**

    | Role | Task |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | **Publications** | Drafts the "Hook" (opening line that makes people click) |
    | **Production** | Verifies technical accuracy (product names, dates, links are correct) |
    | **Client SPOC** | Approves final caption before posting |

    **Best Practices:**
    - Hook should be 1–2 sentences, punchy
    - Include relevant hashtags (production provides suggestions)
    - Test links before publishing
    - Schedule post during peak engagement hours
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### 🟢 Post-Production FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Why can't Production just post the video directly?">
    Publications specializes in audience engagement, SEO, and platform algorithms. Production ensures the asset is technically perfect; Publications ensures it is *seen*. Different expertise, same goal.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How many rounds of revisions are included in the project scope?">
    Standard: 2 rounds per stage (Assembly + Fine Cut = 4 total). Additional rounds are change orders.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How long should we keep raw footage after a project is published?">
    **Raw footage:** 30 days post-publication, then archive to cold storage.
    **Master export:** Archive indefinitely (you never know when a client wants a re-edit).
    **Project files:** Keep for 1 year, then archive (space/server costs).
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>

## Summary: The Production Pipeline Mindset

**Pre-Pre** = Filter (Is this viable?)
**Pre-Prod** = Blueprint (What are we making?)
**On-Site** = Execute (Make it happen)
**Post-Prod** = Refine (Make it perfect)
**Publication** = Release (Let the world see it)

Each phase has **decision gates**. You cannot move forward without sign-off from the previous phase. This structure prevents the chaos of reactive filmmaking and enables the department to scale.

---

## Key Principles

1. **Document everything.** A thought in someone's head is worthless; a thought in a shared doc is a guideline.
2. **Standardize processes.** Different crews should produce consistent output.
3. **Plan obsessively.** The more you plan, the less you improvise on-set, the less you re-shoot in post.
4. **Communicate like a production.** Use call sheets, shot lists, and run-of-shows. Formality saves time.
5. **Safety first, always.** Crew health > creative vision. Always.
