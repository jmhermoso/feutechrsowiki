# Setup and Strike

Setup and Strike are the bookends of every production — the moments where months of planning meet the physical reality of a venue, and where a well-built set either comes together cleanly or reveals every shortcut that was taken during fabrication.

## Installation and Cable Management

On-site setup is where the Set Design and Technicals departments converge. Coordination between both teams during load-in is essential to avoid rework.

<Steps>
  ### Placement
  Position all set pieces according to the venue scout notes. Confirm sightlines and clearances before committing to final positions — moving a large flat after cables are run wastes significant time.

  ### Cable Hiding
  Route all power and signal cables through the pre-built channels in the set structure. Use gaff tape and **beauty panels** to conceal any remaining exposed wiring. On camera, zero loose cables should be visible.

  ### The Camera Test
  Before declaring setup complete, view the full set through the actual production cameras — not a phone or a preview monitor. Check for **hot spots** (unwanted reflections from set surfaces or fixtures) and unintended shadows. Adjust set dressing, lighting, or camera position as needed until the frame is clean.
</Steps>

<Callout type="info" title="The Camera Test is mandatory">
  Setup is not complete until the Camera Test has been done and signed off by the Technical Director or their designee. Do not begin rehearsals on an untested set.
</Callout>

## Strike and Storage

A set represents an investment of both money and carbon. Striking it carelessly destroys that investment. The Strike Phase must be as deliberate and systematic as the build.

<Callout type="warn" title="Deconstruction, Not Destruction">
  Set pieces are not disposable. Smashing or forcing apart panels ruins the material for future student projects. Disassemble carefully — the goal is components that can be reused next season.
</Callout>

The strike process follows a consistent order:

- **Remove fasteners first** — unscrew and unbolt all connections systematically. Save all hardware in labeled bins sorted by type and size.
- **Separate by reuse category** — set pieces that will be stored go to the Designated Storage area; materials that cannot be reused go to the appropriate recycling or waste stream.
- **Label everything going into storage** — large set pieces must be labeled with the production name, date, and dimensions before they are stacked. An unlabeled piece is effectively lost to the next team.
- **Store in a dry environment** — wood and cardboard components must be kept off the floor and away from moisture to prevent warping and mold.

## FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="What is gaff tape and why not use regular tape?">
    Gaff tape is a heavy-duty, cloth-backed tape with a matte finish that does not reflect light on camera. Regular tape (masking, duct, packing) reflects light and leaves adhesive residue on surfaces and floors. Always use gaff tape for on-set cable management and set dressing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What counts as a 'hot spot' during the camera test?">
    A hot spot is any area of the frame that appears unnaturally bright due to a reflective surface catching a light source. Common causes are glossy paint finishes, metallic hardware left visible on set, and shiny flooring. Dull the surface with matte spray, reposition the offending light, or mask the reflection with set dressing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Who is responsible for calling the end of strike?">
    The Fabrication Lead confirms that all set pieces are properly stored or disposed of. The Logistics Officer does a final walkthrough of the venue to confirm nothing is left behind and signs off on equipment return. Strike is not complete until both have cleared the space.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if a set piece is damaged during strike?">
    Document the damage with photos immediately. Log it in the physical inventory with a note on the condition so the next team is not surprised. If the damage is repairable, tag the piece for repair before storage. If it is beyond use, dispose of it responsibly — do not store broken pieces that cannot be salvaged.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
