# Design and Budget

The pre-phase is a cyclical process of aligning creative ambition with financial and physical reality. This phase lives entirely on paper and screen — no tools are picked up, no materials are ordered, and no budget is spent until the design has cleared the full approval loop.

<Callout type="warn" title="Hard Stop">
  Fabrication does not begin until both the Final Render and the Cost Sheet carry a sign-off. Proceeding without approval is a process violation.
</Callout>

## Initial Brief and Creative Concept

The process opens with the **Client Brief**, where the desired look, mood, and functional requirements of the set are established. From there, the design team produces **Sketches** and **Digital Renders** to visualize lighting, textures, and depth before any physical commitment is made.

When producing renders, keep these two constraints front of mind:

- **Student-First Design** — Renders must be achievable with basic hand tools. Avoid complex joinery that requires specialized or industrial machinery.
- **Camera Testing** — Renders must account for actual camera angles, ensuring, for example, that a backdrop is wide enough to fill a Medium-Wide shot without exposing the edges of the set.

## Background and Printable Sizing Reference

Choosing the right background size must happen at the render stage — not on load-in day. The sizes below are standard commercial references. Always check inventory first before ordering; if a suitable piece is not in stock, submit a request through the Logistics Officer.

<Callout type="info" title="Tarpaulin Pricing">
  Tarpaulin banners are charged by area at a standard rate of **₱20/ft²**. A 2×5 ft banner costs ₱200 to print. Factor this into your cost sheet early — large backdrops add up fast.
</Callout>

## The Approval and Revision Loop

Set design is rarely approved on the first draft. The team must navigate a structured revision cycle until the client and budget both align.

<Steps>
  <Step>
    ### Initial Rendering
    Present the digital mockup alongside an estimated cost sheet. Include material quantities, unit costs, printable dimensions, and a contingency buffer.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    ### Client Review
    The client provides feedback on aesthetics, functionality, or budget constraints. Document all feedback in writing so revision scope is clear.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    ### Revision
    Adjust material choices or reduce design complexity to meet constraints. If a significant design element must be cut, flag it explicitly to the client — do not quietly remove it.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    ### Final Sign-off
    Once both the Final Render and the Cost Sheet are approved and signed, fabrication may begin. No materials are purchased before this step.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="What counts as a 'Client Brief'?">
    The client brief is a written or verbal statement from the project lead or event owner describing the desired visual tone, the venue, the event type, and any hard constraints (budget ceiling, load-in time, storage limitations). It does not need to be a formal document, but its contents must be recorded before design work begins.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can we skip the revision loop if the client approves the first render?">
    Yes — if the first render is approved and the cost sheet is signed, you may proceed directly to fabrication. The loop exists to handle revisions, not to create mandatory waiting periods.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Who is authorized to give final sign-off?">
    The Production Lead provides final sign-off on the design, and the Logistics Officer countersigns the cost sheet to confirm budget availability. Both signatures are required before any purchase order is raised.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do I know what backdrop size to use before I see the venue?">
    Use your widest planned shot (typically a Medium-Wide or Wide) as the baseline. A subject standing 6–10 ft from the backdrop generally needs at least an 8×10 ft background to fill the frame safely. When in doubt, size up — excess backdrop can be folded or flagged off; a backdrop that is too small cannot be fixed on shoot day.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What software should we use for digital renders?">
    There is no mandated tool. Common options include Canva (for quick mood boards), SketchUp Free (for 3D spatial mockups), and Adobe Illustrator or Photoshop (for detailed 2D renders). Use whatever your team can operate confidently — the output quality matters more than the tool.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
