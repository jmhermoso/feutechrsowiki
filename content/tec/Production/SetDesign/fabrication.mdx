# Fabrication

Fabrication is the transition from digital pixels to physical matter. In our organization, the build phase is viewed as a learning opportunity — officers gain practical engineering and construction skills while producing a set that can serve the organization for multiple years.

<Callout type="info" title="Design approval is a prerequisite">
  This phase begins only after the Final Render and Cost Sheet have been signed. See the Design and Budget page if sign-off is still pending.
</Callout>

## Sustainable Building Ideology

We prioritize the **Circular Economy** in our fabrication process. The goal is to build sets that can be reused, reconfigured, and eventually recycled — not discarded after a single event.

<Tabs items={['Recycled Materials', 'Modular Systems', 'Low-Impact Finishes']}>
  <Tab value="Recycled Materials">
    Prioritize reclaimed and repurposed materials wherever possible:

    - **Reclaimed wood pallets** for structural framing and rustic surface treatments
    - **Cardboard** for full-scale mockups and lightweight backing panels
    - **Repurposed plastic scraps** cut and shaped into light diffusers

    Before purchasing new materials, check the Workshop Locker and storage inventory for usable stock from previous productions.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Modular Systems">
    Build in standard, reusable sizes rather than one-off custom dimensions:

    - Standard flat size: **4ft × 8ft** — matches common material sheet dimensions and is movable by two students
    - Frames built to this standard can be **reconfigured and repainted** across multiple productions without rebuilding from scratch
    - Document all modular pieces in the physical inventory after each strike so the next team knows what is available

    This approach is our "LEGO system" — individual pieces that compose into different sets each season.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Low-Impact Finishes">
    Choose finishes that allow future disassembly and material recovery:

    - **Water-based paints** over solvent-based alternatives — easier cleanup and lower VOC emissions
    - **Mechanical fasteners** (screws and bolts) over permanent adhesives wherever structurally feasible
    - Avoid hot glue or construction adhesive on structural members — these prevent disassembly and ruin the material for future use
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Grip Reference

"Grip" refers to the equipment used to mount, suspend, position, and stabilize set pieces, lights, and backdrops. Knowing what grip exists — and its limitations — prevents both on-site improvisation and unsafe setups. Always check inventory or submit a request before assuming a piece of grip is available.

<Callout type="warn" title="Respect load ratings">
  Every grip item has a rated load limit. Exceeding it is a safety hazard, not just an equipment concern. When in doubt, use a heavier-duty solution or distribute the load across multiple points.
</Callout>

<Tabs items={['Stands', 'Clamps and Arms', 'Rigging and Suspension', 'Sandbags and Ballast']}>
  <Tab value="Stands">
    | Type | Common Use | Limitations |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Light stand** | Mounting lights, small reflectors | Not for heavy fixtures; tips easily without ballast |
    | **C-stand (Century stand)** | Holding flags, nets, arms, small set pieces | Heavier and more stable than a light stand; still requires sandbag on the low leg |
    | **Backdrop stand (crossbar system)** | Supporting fabric or paper backdrops | Width-limited by crossbar length; not for tarpaulins over 8 ft wide without additional support |
    | **T-bar / overhead rig** | Suspending backdrops from above | Requires ceiling attachment point or freestanding goal-post frame; check venue ceiling height |

    **General rule:** Any stand holding something above head height must be sandbagged regardless of how stable it feels unweighted.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Clamps and Arms">
    | Type | Common Use | Limitations |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Cardellini clamp** | Attaching to pipes, flat surfaces, lumber | Rated for moderate loads; not for suspending heavy fixtures overhead |
    | **Mafer clamp** | Gripping irregular surfaces, scaffolding | Strong grip but can mar soft surfaces — use padding |
    | **Gaffer clamp (A-clamp)** | Quick, lightweight attachments | Light duty only — signage, small reflectors, cable routing |
    | **Magic arm** | Positioning monitors, small lights, camera accessories | Weight-rated per manufacturer; tighten all joints fully before loading |
    | **Crossbar / boom arm** | Extending a light or flag horizontally off a stand | Increases leverage — counterweight with sandbag on opposite side |

  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Rigging and Suspension">
    Overhead rigging is the highest-risk grip category. Unless your team has trained riggers, limit overhead work to lightweight items (fabric backdrops, small LED fixtures) on rated truss or pipe systems.

    | Item | Use | Notes |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Truss / pipe and base** | Overhead backdrop or lighting bar | Must be rated for total suspended load; inspect before each use |
    | **Safety cable / safety bond** | Backup retention for any overhead fixture | Mandatory for anything suspended above people — no exceptions |
    | **Ratchet strap** | Tensioning fabric backdrops across a frame | Not a primary structural fastener; use for tensioning only |
    | **Bungee / shock cord** | Lightweight backdrop tensioning | For light fabric only; never use for anything with weight |

    <Callout type="warn" title="Safety cables are not optional">
      Any fixture or set piece suspended overhead must have an independent safety cable attached to a rated anchor point. This applies even when the primary mount feels secure.
    </Callout>
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Sandbags and Ballast">
    Sandbags are not optional accessories — they are safety equipment. A toppled stand is a liability.

    - Standard sandbag weight: **15–25 lbs** — always place on the lowest leg of a C-stand or the base of any loaded light stand
    - For tall stands or stands with boom arms extended, use **two sandbags**
    - Water-fillable ballast bags are an acceptable alternative where weight transport is a concern
    - Never substitute improvised ballast (camera bags, personal items) for proper sandbags — they shift and are unreliable
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Blueprinting and Assembly Guides

Digital renders communicate the look of a set. They do not communicate how to build it. Before any cutting or assembly begins, the Fabrication Lead must produce **Technical Drawings** — dimensioned plans that a student with no prior context can follow.

A complete set of technical drawings must include:

- **Exact dimensions** for every panel, frame, and structural member
- **Joinery specifications** — prefer Dry-Fit methods; use bolts and wingnuts wherever possible so the set can be assembled and disassembled on-site without power tools
- **Cable paths** — hidden channels for power and signal cables must be drawn into the blueprint so they are built into the structure, not taped on afterward
- **Grip points** — indicate where stands, clamps, or rigging will attach to the set so these are accounted for in the structural design

<Callout type="warn" title="Renders are not blueprints">
  A digital render approved by the client is a visual reference only. Do not begin cutting materials from a render. Always produce a dimensioned technical drawing first.
</Callout>

## FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="What is the best lightweight material for backdrops?">
    **Coroplast (Corrugated Plastic)** is the recommended choice. It is light enough for one student to carry, waterproof, and can be cleanly recycled after use. It also accepts paint well when primed with a plastic-adhesion primer.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if a set piece ends up too heavy for the crew to move?">
    This is a design failure that must be caught at the blueprint stage, not on load-in day. All set pieces must be either Man-Portable (movable by two students without mechanical assistance) or built on heavy-duty locking casters. If a piece exceeds this threshold, redesign it before cutting begins.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What grip should I use to hang a tarpaulin backdrop?">
    For tarps up to 8×10 ft, a crossbar backdrop stand system with the tarp grommeted and tied at even intervals is the standard approach. For wider tarps, use a pipe-and-base overhead rig or a goal-post frame, and tension the bottom edge with ratchet straps or bungees to a floor anchor to prevent billowing. Always sandbag the stands.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do we need to document every build step?">
    For recurring modular pieces, yes. A short photo log or annotated diagram of the assembly sequence should be stored with the set piece in storage. This allows future teams to reassemble the piece correctly without needing to involve the original builder.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can we use power tools on-site during load-in?">
    Only if the venue permits it and proper PPE is available. The preferred approach is to pre-assemble as much as possible in the workshop so that on-site work is limited to bolt-together connections. This reduces noise, dust, and load-in time.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
