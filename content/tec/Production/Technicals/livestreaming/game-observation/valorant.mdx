# Valorant

Valorant is a 5v5 tactical first-person shooter developed by Riot Games. Players are assigned to one of two sides — **Attackers** and **Defenders** — and switch sides at halftime. Each agent has a unique set of abilities that complement the team's strategy. The core format is round-based with no respawns within a round.

<Callout type="warn" title="The Spike is not a character ability">
  In Valorant, the Spike is the bomb — it is the primary objective of the standard game mode. It is carried by one attacker per round and planted at a designated site. Do not confuse it with ability terminology from other games.
</Callout>

## Game Modes

<Tabs items={['Standard (Unrated / Competitive)', 'Spike Rush', 'Team Deathmatch', 'Deathmatch', 'Escalation']}>
  <Tab value="Standard (Unrated / Competitive)">
    The primary competitive format. Attackers attempt to plant the Spike at Site A or Site B; defenders prevent the plant or defuse after a successful plant. First team to 13 rounds wins. Overtime is played in pairs of rounds if tied at 12–12.

    **Key callouts:**

    | Term | Meaning |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | **Spike** | The bomb — carried by one attacker and planted at a site to win the round |
    | **Site A / Site B / Mid** | The three primary zones on most maps — A and B are plant sites, Mid is the contested center |
    | **Plant** | When the Spike carrier begins arming the Spike at a site (4 seconds to complete) |
    | **Defuse** | When a defender disarms a planted Spike (7 seconds; can be interrupted) |
    | **Post-plant** | The phase after the Spike is planted — attackers defend the Spike, defenders push to defuse |
    | **Retake** | Defenders pushing back onto a site after attackers have planted and established control |
    | **Clutch** | One player remaining alive in a round against multiple opponents |
    | **Eco round** | A round where one team purchases minimal weapons to preserve economy |
    | **Force buy** | Purchasing weapons despite low economy, risking future round resources |
    | **Half / Switch** | At round 12, teams swap sides — attackers become defenders and vice versa |
    | **Operator (Op)** | The AWP-equivalent sniper rifle — one-shot kill, high cost, changes site control dynamics |
    | **Utility / Abilities** | Each agent's non-ultimate abilities — smokes, flashes, molotovs, recon |
    | **Ultimate** | Each agent's most powerful ability, charged by kills, deaths, and orbs |
    | **Orb** | Two orbs on each map that charge ultimates when collected |

    **Observer priorities by phase:**

    | Phase | Focus |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | **Buy phase** | Skip or cut to team composition view — no action |
    | **Early round (attack)** | Follow the Spike carrier or the team's primary entry fraggers |
    | **Site execute** | Cut to wide angle showing the full site push — ability usage is the story |
    | **Plant moment** | Stay on the Spike plant — this is the round pivot |
    | **Post-plant** | Show the crossfire positions attackers take to defend the Spike |
    | **Retake** | Follow the defending team pushing back onto the site |
    | **Clutch** | Lock on the surviving player — do not cut away regardless of how slow it gets |
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Spike Rush">
    Spike Rush is a shorter, faster format. Every attacker starts with the Spike (all five carry it). Orbs spawn on the map granting random power-ups. First team to four round wins.

    **Key differences from Standard:**
    - All attackers carry the Spike — any of them can plant at any time
    - Rounds are shorter and more chaotic — action escalates faster
    - **Orbs** grant powerful temporary effects (Golden Gun, Deception Orb, Paranoia Orb) — treat an orb pickup as a broadcast moment
    - No economy — all players receive the same weapon each round

    **Observer priorities:**
    - Stay in the action — Spike Rush has very little downtime
    - **Orb fights** are often more important than the actual Spike plant — follow them
    - The lack of economy means aggressive plays are more frequent — anticipate flanks and aggressive pushes
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Team Deathmatch">
    Team Deathmatch is a respawn-enabled mode where teams compete to reach a kill limit first. Players respawn after a short delay.

    **Key callouts:**

    | Term | Meaning |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | **Kill count** | Current score toward the match limit — displayed on the HUD |
    | **Respawn** | Time between death and re-entering the game |
    | **Power-up zones** | Designated areas on the TDM map that grant ability charges |

    **Observer priorities:**
    - Follow the highest-kill player on the leading team
    - Cut to **multi-kill sequences** immediately — these are the highlights
    - Unlike standard modes, there is no single pivot moment — the broadcast is about individual skill expression
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Deathmatch">
    Free-for-all Deathmatch. Every player is an opponent. First to reach the kill limit wins. Used primarily as a warm-up mode.

    **Observer note:** Deathmatch is rarely the focus of a broadcast event. If it is being covered (e.g. a warm-up segment), treat it as individual highlight coverage — follow the player currently on a kill streak.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Escalation">
    Escalation is a team-based mode where both teams progress through a shared sequence of weapons. Getting kills with the current weapon advances the team to the next weapon tier. First team to complete all weapon tiers, or the team furthest through the tiers when time expires, wins.

    **Key concept:** The current required weapon is the story. When a team is one kill away from advancing, that kill is the broadcast moment.

    **Observer priorities:**
    - Track which weapon tier each team is on via the HUD
    - Follow the player closest to securing the advancement kill
    - The **final tier** (typically the knife) is always the most exciting — stay close and personal
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## FAQ

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Should I follow a flank in a post-plant situation?">
    Only if the flank has a realistic path to defuse. In post-plant, the story is the Spike timer and the crossfire. If a defender is making a clean approach that could reach the Spike, follow them — that is the tension. If they are clearly caught out of position with no path, stay on the main fight.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What is the most important moment to capture in a standard round?">
    The plant moment and the clutch, in that order. The plant is the round pivot — everything changes after it. If the round reaches a clutch situation, that player becomes the entire broadcast focus until the round ends.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do I show ability usage clearly for viewers who don't know the game?">
    Zoom out slightly when an ultimate is activating so the full effect is visible in frame. For utility like smokes and flashes, a wider angle that shows both the ability and the players reacting to it tells the story better than a close-up on the ability alone.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
