# Section 1: Content Posting and Creative Workflow

This section serves as the operational manual for the creative pipeline. It moves from high-level discovery to the granular details of technical handover. By following this architecture, the department maintains a standardized quality of output regardless of which officer is leading the project.

---

## 1.1 Pre-Pre: The Discovery Phase

The discovery phase acts as the filter for the entire department. It is designed to prevent resource mismanagement by ensuring a project is viable before any technical labor begins.

### Understanding the Request and Client Intake
The process is initiated via a formal request. To maintain clear communication, the production lead must identify a **Single Point of Contact (SPOC)**. This prevents "instruction creep" where multiple voices from a client organization provide conflicting directions.

| Requirement | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| **SPOC Identification** | Prevents conflicting revisions and communication bottlenecks. |
| **Core Objective** | Defines if the content is for archival, promotion, or live broadcast. |
| **Visual References** | 2-3 external links to align on the expected "look and feel." |

### Scope Definition and Limitations
With the objective clear, the lead must define the boundaries of the project. Establishing the "Creative Freedom" vs. "Client Mandates" at this stage prevents friction during the editing process. A critical component here is the **Scope Creep Policy**, which stipulates that any request changing the workload by more than 20% after initial approval must be processed as a separate project.

### Research and Feasibility
The final step of the discovery phase is a cold assessment of the department's current capabilities. This involves a hardware audit to ensure specific gear—like high-speed cameras or specialized lenses—is available, followed by a location audit to identify environmental hazards such as poor acoustics or power limitations.

---

### 🟢 Pre-Pre FAQ
> **How do we handle a client who refuses to provide a SPOC?**
> Inform the client that without a single point of contact, the production timeline will be extended to account for conflicting revisions, and the "Kill Switch" policy will be strictly enforced.
>
> **What if the request exceeds our current equipment's capabilities?**
> Flag this in the feasibility report. The project may proceed only if the client provides the budget for rentals or accepts a lower technical tier of delivery.

## 1.2 Pre-Production: The Blueprint

Pre-production is the phase where abstract ideas are converted into actionable technical data. Without a blueprint, on-site execution becomes reactive rather than proactive.

### Creative Assets and Storyboarding
Visualizing the project is the most effective way to save time during a shoot. The team begins by drafting a **Logline**, a single sentence that keeps the narrative focused. This is followed by **Moodboarding** to align on aesthetic choices like color palettes and lighting. Finally, **Storyboarding** provides a sequential map of every planned shot.

### Logistics and Planning
Technical planning involves a physical or virtual **Venue Scout**. During this walkthrough, the lead marks camera positions, identifies the nearest power circuits, and maps out cable runs. Parallel to the scout, the team conducts **Talent Sourcing**, ensuring all on-camera personnel are briefed on the schedule and wardrobe requirements.

### Equipment Manifest
The final output of logistics is the **Equipment Manifest**. This is an itemized list of every component required for the shoot. Every item, down to the specific lens adapters and SD card speeds, must be accounted for to prevent on-site delays.

---

### 🟢 Pre-Production FAQ
> **Why do we need a moodboard if the client already gave us a reference video?**
> A reference video shows what someone else did; a moodboard shows what *we* will do. It translates the client’s vague "vibe" into specific lighting and color targets our team can hit.
>
> **Is a venue scout necessary for a location we have used before?**
> Yes. Facilities change, power outlets may be broken, or new furniture may obstruct previous camera angles. Always verify the site 24–48 hours prior.

## 1.3 On-Site: The Shoot

The on-site phase is characterized by strict adherence to the schedule and the physical safety of the crew and equipment.

### Production Documentation
Two documents govern the shoot: the **Callsheet** and the **Shotlist**. The Callsheet is the logistical heart of the day, containing the location, weather, and emergency contacts. The Shotlist is the operational heart, acting as a checklist to ensure that no "non-negotiable" shots are missed.

### The Production Timeline (Run-of-Show)
To maintain order, the team follows a standard Run-of-Show. This workflow ensures that technical foundations are laid before creative talent arrives, minimizing downtime for expensive personnel.

| Phase | Activity | Operational Priority |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Load-In** | Equipment arrival and staging | Inventory and safety check |
| **Tech Setup** | Cable runs, signal testing, lighting | Signal stability and sync |
| **Shoot Window** | Active recording/broadcasting | Adherence to the shotlist |
| **Strike** | Systematic teardown | Gear recovery and site cleanup |

### Crew Management
Between the technical phases, the lead must manage **Rest and Meal Breaks**. Production efficiency drops significantly after five hours of continuous labor; scheduling these breaks is a technical necessity to prevent mistakes.

---

### 🟢 On-Site FAQ
> **What is the priority if we are running behind schedule?**
> Refer to the "Non-negotiables" in the Shotlist. Drop the "nice-to-have" creative shots and focus entirely on the core deliverables required by the client.
>
> **Who is responsible for gear safety on-site?**
> Every crew member is responsible for their immediate station, but the Lead is responsible for the final "Litter Sweep" and inventory check during the Strike phase.

## 1.4 Post-Production and Publication

Post-production focuses on data integrity and the final departmental "handshake" that brings the content to the audience.

### File Organization and Ingest
The workflow begins with **Data Ingest**. Footage must be moved to a master drive with an immediate secondary backup. We utilize a standardized naming convention to ensure any editor can step into the project and understand the file structure immediately.

* **Format:** `YYYYMMDD_ProjectName_ClipDescription_V01`
* **Structure:** `01_RAW`, `02_AUDIO`, `03_ASSETS`, `04_PROJECT_FILES`, `05_EXPORTS`

### The Editing Workflow
Editing proceeds in two major stages: the **Assembly Cut** and the **Fine Cut**. The Assembly focuses on story and pacing, while the Fine Cut introduces color grading and sound design. To prevent endless revision cycles, all client feedback must be logged using timestamped comments.

### The Publication Handshake
Once the edit is finalized, the Production department facilitates the handover to the **Publications Department**. Production provides the high-quality masters and "social cut-downs" (9:16), while Publications manages the distribution.

### Collaborative Publication Guide
1.  **The Handover Package:** Production uploads assets to the shared drive.
2.  **Context Briefing:** Production provides a document with correct name spellings and technical warnings.
3.  **Caption Verification:** Publications drafts the "Hook"; Production verifies the technical accuracy of the copy.

---

### 🟢 Post-Production FAQ
> **Why can't Production just post the video directly?**
> Publications specializes in audience engagement, SEO, and platform algorithms. Production’s job is to ensure the asset is technically perfect; Publications' job is to ensure it is seen.
>
> **How long should we keep raw footage after a project is published?**
> Standard practice is to keep raw footage for 30 days post-publication, while the Master Export is archived indefinitely.
