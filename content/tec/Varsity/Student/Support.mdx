## Recognizing Signs of Stress & Burnout

As a manager, you are often the first to notice when the pressure is getting to be too much. Be aware of these potential indicators:

* **Behavioral Changes:** Increased irritability, withdrawal from the team, or a sudden lack of motivation.
* **Physical Complaints:** Frequent headaches, visible fatigue, or neglecting personal hygiene.
* **Performance Declines:** A noticeable drop in in-game focus or a sudden inability to keep up with team responsibilities.
* **Expressed Distress:** Feelings of being overwhelmed or constant mentions of excessive stress.

## Connecting Players with Professional Resources

Your job is not to fix the problem yourself, but to guide players toward the people who can.

* **Campus Health Services:** Refer players here for physical health concerns or persistent fatigue.
* **Counseling and Psychological Services (CAPS):** Guide players to University Counseling for mental health support, stress management, and burnout. 
    * *Note:* Remind players that these services are strictly **confidential**.
* **Trusted Mentors:** Encourage players to speak with the **Organizational Adviser** or another trusted staff member if they are struggling.

## Confidentiality & Discretion

Handling well-being issues requires a high degree of sensitivity.

### Respect Privacy
When a player shares personal health or wellness information, it stays with you. Maintain absolute confidentiality to build and keep their trust.

### Report Only on a "Need-to-Know" Basis
Information should only be shared with leadership (Head Varsity Manager, Adviser, or VPI) if it impacts:
* The player’s immediate safety.
* Their ability to continue participating in the varsity.
* A requirement for official university accommodations.

### Refer, Don't Diagnose
**This is critical:** Your role is to observe, support, and refer. You are a manager, not a counselor or medical professional. Always point them to the professionals at CAPS or Health Services.
